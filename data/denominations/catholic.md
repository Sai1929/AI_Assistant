# Roman Catholic Tradition — Doctrinal Positions

This document summarises the Roman Catholic Church's teaching on fifteen contested theological topics. Sources cited include the *Catechism of the Catholic Church* (CCC), ecumenical council documents, and papal encyclicals.

---

## 1. Marian Dogmas

The Catholic Church holds four Marian dogmas as divinely revealed truths: Mary's Divine Motherhood (Council of Ephesus, 431), her Perpetual Virginity, her Immaculate Conception (defined 1854, *Ineffabilis Deus*), and her Assumption body and soul into heaven (defined 1950, *Munificentissimus Deus*). These dogmas are understood not as additions to the Gospel but as developments of what was implicitly contained in the deposit of faith from the apostles (CCC 487–511). Mary is honoured as *Theotokos* (God-bearer) and *Mediatrix*, roles that Catholics regard as inseparable from her unique cooperation in the work of redemption. The veneration (Greek *hyperdulia*) offered to Mary is carefully distinguished from the worship (*latria*) given to God alone (CCC 971).

## 2. Sacraments

The Catholic Church recognises seven sacraments instituted by Christ: Baptism, Confirmation, Eucharist, Penance, Anointing of the Sick, Holy Orders, and Matrimony (CCC 1113). Each sacrament is an efficacious sign that confers the grace it signifies (*ex opere operato*), meaning the sacrament's effect does not depend on the personal holiness of the minister but on Christ's action through the rite (CCC 1128). The sacramental system is understood as the ordinary means by which God distributes saving grace within the Church. Valid administration requires proper matter, form, and intention, as defined by Church teaching and canon law.

## 3. Justification

Catholic teaching holds that justification is a process of interior renewal and sanctification, not merely a legal declaration (Council of Trent, Session 6, 1547). Saving faith must be accompanied by hope and charity; "faith alone" (*sola fide*) is rejected as an incomplete account of how sinners are made righteous before God (CCC 1987–2005). Grace is understood as a real participation in the divine life infused into the soul, enabling meritorious acts that contribute to one's ongoing justification. The Catholic-Lutheran *Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification* (1999) identified important areas of convergence while acknowledging remaining differences on the role of merit and the nature of righteousness.

## 4. Predestination

The Catholic Church affirms that God predestines the elect to eternal life, but resolutely rejects double predestination—the idea that God positively wills the damnation of the reprobate (CCC 600, 1037). All human beings are created for eternal happiness and are offered sufficient grace for salvation; damnation results from the creature's free rejection of God, not from divine decree. The precise relationship between divine foreknowledge, predestination, and human freedom has been the subject of ongoing theological debate (the *de Auxiliis* controversy between Dominicans and Jesuits), and the Church has not defined a single school of thought as binding. Catholics hold together God's absolute sovereignty and genuine human freedom without fully resolving the philosophical tension.

## 5. Scriptural Canon

The Catholic Bible includes 73 books: the 27 books of the New Testament plus 46 books of the Old Testament, which incorporates seven deuterocanonical books (Tobit, Judith, 1–2 Maccabees, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch) not found in the Hebrew Masoretic canon (CCC 120; Council of Trent, 1546). These books were present in the Septuagint (LXX), the Greek Old Testament widely used by the early Church and New Testament authors. The Church received and recognised this canon at regional councils (Hippo 393, Carthage 397, 419) and definitively defined it at Trent in response to Protestant challenges. Catholics regard the determination of the canon itself as evidence that the Church's living Tradition and teaching authority precede and authenticate Scripture.

## 6. Ecclesiology

The Catholic Church understands itself as the one Church of Christ, which "subsists in" the Catholic Church governed by the successor of Peter and the bishops in communion with him (*Lumen Gentium* 8, Vatican II). The Pope, as Bishop of Rome and successor of Peter, possesses the fullness of episcopal power and serves as the visible principle of unity for the whole Church (CCC 880–882). Other Christian communities are seen as possessing elements of the true Church in varying degrees, and the separated brethren are regarded as brothers and sisters in Christ, even if full visible unity is lacking (*Unitatis Redintegratio*, Vatican II). The Church is simultaneously hierarchical and charismatic, institutional and Spirit-filled, local and universal.

## 7. Eschatology

Catholic teaching affirms the resurrection of the body, the particular judgment of each soul immediately after death, heaven, purgatory, hell, and the final Last Judgment (CCC 1020–1060). Heaven is the beatific vision—the direct knowledge and enjoyment of God as he is in himself—granted to those who die in God's grace and friendship (CCC 1023). Hell is a definitive self-exclusion from communion with God, eternally chosen by those who persist in mortal sin until death (CCC 1033). Purgatory is a state of final purification for those who die in God's grace but still require cleansing from the temporal effects of sin before entering heaven (CCC 1030–1032).

## 8. Baptism

Baptism is the first and foundational sacrament, by which a person is incorporated into Christ and the Church, receives the forgiveness of original and personal sins, and is reborn as a child of God (CCC 1213–1284). The Catholic Church practises infant baptism, viewing it as consistent with God's covenant initiative and with the apostolic tradition attested by Church Fathers such as Origen and Cyprian. Baptism by water is the ordinary means of salvation, though the Church recognises baptism of desire and baptism of blood for those who cannot receive the sacrament. Valid baptism—with water in the Trinitarian formula—is recognised across most Christian denominations, reflecting the Church's affirmation that baptism unites all Christians in a real, though imperfect, bond.

## 9. Eucharist

The Eucharist is the "source and summit of the Christian life" (CCC 1324; *Lumen Gentium* 11). The Catholic Church teaches that at the words of consecration the bread and wine are substantially transformed into the Body and Blood of Christ—a change traditionally called transubstantiation (CCC 1376; Council of Trent). Christ is thus truly, really, and substantially present under the eucharistic species, not merely symbolically or spiritually. The Mass is also understood as the unbloody re-presentation of the one sacrifice of Calvary, not a new or additional sacrifice, making the saving act of Christ present across time to each celebrating community.

## 10. Papal Authority

The Pope exercises supreme, full, immediate, and universal authority over the whole Church by virtue of his office as successor of Peter (CCC 882; Vatican I, *Pastor Aeternus*, 1870). When speaking *ex cathedra* on matters of faith and morals with the intention of binding the whole Church, the Pope is preserved from error by the charism of infallibility—a gift of the Holy Spirit to safeguard the deposit of faith (CCC 891). This infallibility has been invoked explicitly only twice: the Immaculate Conception (1854) and the Assumption (1950). Ordinary papal teaching, while not always infallible, demands the "religious assent of intellect and will" from Catholics (CCC 892).

## 11. Scripture and Tradition

Catholic teaching holds that Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition together constitute one sacred deposit of the Word of God, entrusted to the Church (CCC 80–83; *Dei Verbum*, Vatican II). Scripture is inspired and inerrant in all that it affirms for the sake of salvation, but it does not contain everything necessary for Christian life and doctrine; Tradition transmits what is not explicitly written (CCC 81). The Magisterium—the Church's living teaching authority—authentically interprets both Scripture and Tradition and is not above them but serves them (CCC 85–86). This three-source model (Scripture, Tradition, Magisterium) is the distinctive Catholic epistemological framework and contrasts with the Protestant principle of *sola Scriptura*.

## 12. Theosis (Divinisation)

Though the term *theosis* is more characteristic of Eastern theology, the Catholic tradition equally affirms that salvation involves a genuine participation in the divine nature (2 Pet 1:4; CCC 460). Theologians such as Thomas Aquinas taught that sanctifying grace is a real sharing in God's own life (*participatio divinae naturae*), elevating the human person to a new supernatural order of being and activity. This divinisation is mediated through the sacraments, prayer, and the practice of virtue, and reaches its completion in the beatific vision in heaven. While the Catholic tradition uses different vocabulary than Eastern Orthodoxy, the underlying conviction that humans are called to share in God's very life is common to both.

## 13. Original Sin

The Catholic Church teaches that Adam's sin was a historical event that resulted in the loss of original holiness and justice for all humanity, transmitted by propagation (CCC 404–409; Council of Trent, Session 5). Original sin is not a personal sin committed by individuals but an inherited condition—a deprivation of original sanctity—that weakens human nature, inclining it toward sin (concupiscence) without being sinful in itself. Baptism removes the guilt of original sin and restores the relationship with God, though the effects of concupiscence and mortality remain in the redeemed life. Augustine of Hippo's extensive writings on original sin profoundly shaped the Western theological tradition on this point.

## 14. Purgatory

Purgatory is a defined Catholic doctrine (Council of Florence, 1439; Council of Trent, 1563): those who die in God's grace but are not yet perfectly purified undergo a process of purification before entering heaven (CCC 1030–1032). The temporal punishment due to sins already forgiven—distinct from their eternal guilt—must be satisfied either in this life through penance and good works or after death through the purifying fire of purgatory. The living faithful can assist the souls in purgatory through prayer, almsgiving, indulgences, and especially the offering of the Mass (CCC 1032). This doctrine rests on the practice of praying for the dead attested in 2 Maccabees 12:46 and in early Christian liturgical texts.

## 15. Hermeneutics

Catholic biblical hermeneutics holds that Scripture must be interpreted within the living Tradition of the Church, attentive to the literal sense (the meaning intended by the human author) and the spiritual senses (allegorical, moral, and anagogical) (*Dei Verbum* 12; CCC 115–118). Historical-critical methods are affirmed as valuable tools for understanding the human dimension of Scripture, but they must be integrated into a hermeneutic of faith that reads the Bible as a unified whole with Christ as its centre (Pontifical Biblical Commission, *The Interpretation of the Bible in the Church*, 1993). The Magisterium reserves the right to define the sense of any passage that pertains to faith and morals, ensuring that private interpretation does not fragment the community of believers. Catholic exegesis thus balances scientific scholarship with the theological conviction that the Holy Spirit guides the Church's reading of its sacred text.
