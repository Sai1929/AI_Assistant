# Eastern Orthodox Tradition — Doctrinal Positions

This document summarises Eastern Orthodox teaching on fifteen contested theological topics. Sources cited include the Seven Ecumenical Councils (325–787 AD), the writings of the Cappadocian Fathers (Basil the Great, Gregory of Nazianzus, Gregory of Nyssa), John of Damascus, Maximus the Confessor, Gregory Palamas, and the *Rudder* (*Pedalion*). The Orthodox Church understands itself not as one denomination among many but as the continuation of the undivided Church of the first millennium.

---

## 1. Marian Dogmas

The Orthodox Church venerates the Theotokos (God-bearer) as the highest of all created beings—"more honourable than the Cherubim and more glorious beyond compare than the Seraphim" (Axion Estin hymn). Her title *Theotokos* was defined at the Council of Ephesus (431) and is central to Orthodox Christology: to say Mary bore God is to confess that the one born of her is truly divine. The Orthodox celebrate the Dormition of the Theotokos (Feast, 15 August), understanding that her body was taken up to heaven after her falling asleep, though the precise mode of her glorification is held reverently without the dogmatic precision of the Roman Catholic definition of the Assumption (1950). The Immaculate Conception is not taught; Orthodox theology does not share the Augustinian account of original sin as transmitted guilt, so the premise of that doctrine does not arise—Mary is honoured as supremely holy by the action of God's grace, not as exempt from original sin in the Western sense.

## 2. Sacraments

Orthodox theology uses the Greek word *mysteria* (mysteries) rather than "sacraments" for the holy rites of the Church, and the traditional number cited is seven (mirroring Rome), though Orthodox theologians such as Alexander Schmemann stress that the entire life of the Church is in some sense sacramental. The mysteries are understood as genuine encounters with the living God through material means, effecting real spiritual transformation; they are not bare signs. The primary mysteries are Baptism, Chrismation (the Orthodox counterpart to Confirmation), and the Eucharist, which together constitute full initiation and are administered together (including to infants). All seven—adding Confession, Holy Unction, Marriage, and Holy Orders—are understood as actions of the Holy Spirit in the Church, not mechanical channels of infused grace operating independently of the recipient's disposition.

## 3. Justification

While Orthodox theology affirms that salvation is entirely by God's grace and not by human merit, the characteristic framework is not forensic justification but *theosis* (deification)—the progressive participation of the human person in the divine life (Athanasius: "He became man so that we might become God"; *On the Incarnation* 54). Orthodox theologians are cautious about the Western legal metaphors of justification, which they see as reducing salvation to a change in one's legal standing before God rather than a transformation of one's nature and person. The death of Christ is understood primarily as the victory over death and the devil (Christus Victor), the recapitulation of humanity in the incarnate Son (Irenaeus), and the offering of the perfect human response to the Father. Repentance (*metanoia*), the sacraments, and ascetic practice are the means through which the saving work of Christ is appropriated by each believer.

## 4. Predestination

Orthodox theology affirms that God in his foreknowledge elects to salvation those who freely respond to his grace, and firmly rejects any notion of double predestination or the irresistible imposition of salvation or damnation (Gregory of Nyssa, *On the Soul and Resurrection*). God's will is that all should be saved (1 Tim 2:4; 2 Pet 3:9), and his grace is truly offered to all; human freedom (*autexousia*), understood not as mere choice but as the image of God (*imago Dei*) in the person, must cooperate with grace through a process called *synergy*. Extreme Augustinian views on predestination—particularly those that lead to double predestination—were never received by the Eastern Church and are regarded as a Western theological deviation. The Orthodox tradition holds the mystery of divine sovereignty and human freedom together without attempting a philosophical resolution, trusting the justice and love of God.

## 5. Scriptural Canon

The Orthodox Old Testament canon generally follows the Septuagint (LXX), the Greek translation used by the New Testament writers and the early Church, which includes books not in the Hebrew Masoretic canon. In practice, most Orthodox churches use a 49-book Old Testament (including Tobit, Judith, 1–4 Maccabees, Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach, Baruch, Psalm 151, and the Prayer of Manasseh), though the precise boundary varies slightly between Greek, Slavic, and other traditions. The New Testament is identical to that of Rome and Protestants: 27 books. Scripture is not separated from the Church's Tradition—the canon itself was established by the Church's conciliar discernment, and Scripture is properly read within the liturgical and patristic context of Tradition.

## 6. Ecclesiology

The Orthodox Church is constituted as a communion of autocephalous (self-governing) local churches—Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, Jerusalem, Moscow, Serbia, Romania, and others—each led by a bishop who is the centre of Eucharistic unity in his diocese. The Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople holds an honorary primacy of honour (*proton en timei*) as "first among equals" but does not possess universal jurisdiction or doctrinal infallibility akin to the Roman Pope (Chalcedonian Canons 28; *Letter of the Eastern Patriarchs*, 1848). Truth is preserved and expressed through the conciliar process (*sobornost*): the consensus of the bishops, received and confirmed by the whole people of God over time, not by any single monarchical authority. The Great Schism of 1054 is understood as the Bishop of Rome's departure from the conciliar model of the undivided Church, rather than as a schism from a pre-existing papal monarchy.

## 7. Eschatology

Orthodox eschatology affirms the resurrection of the body, the Last Judgment, eternal life with God, and eternal separation from God (Gehenna) (John of Damascus, *An Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith* 4.27). The intermediate state is one in which souls experience a foretaste of their final destiny—the righteous at rest and anticipating the joy of God, the unrighteous in a condition of conscious suffering—while awaiting the general resurrection and judgment. Purgatory as defined by Rome (a place of satisfactory punishment for temporal penalties) is not taught; rather, the Orthodox pray for the dead at every Divine Liturgy and on special Saturdays of the Dead (*Saturdays of the Souls*), believing that God's mercy can work in ways beyond human understanding. The ultimate destiny of the reprobate is guarded with theological reserve; some Orthodox theologians (e.g., Origen's later influence, St. Gregory of Nyssa, and more recently Sergius Bulgakov) have expressed hope for universal salvation (*apokatastasis*), though this remains a private theological opinion, not a dogma.

## 8. Baptism

Baptism is the mystery of new birth by which a person is grafted into Christ and the Church, dying and rising with Christ (Rom 6:3–5; John 3:5), and it is administered by triple immersion in the name of the Trinity (the *Rudder* is emphatic on triple immersion as normative). Infants are baptised immediately followed by Chrismation and reception of Holy Communion, completing full sacramental initiation in a single rite—reflecting the conviction that grace precedes and enables faith. Reception of converts from other Christian communities varies by local practice: some (like Rome or Protestant traditions using valid Trinitarian baptism) are received by Chrismation alone; others are received by full baptism, reflecting different assessments of sacramental validity outside the canonical Church. Baptism by the Holy Spirit (Acts 2) is understood as inseparable from water baptism in Orthodox sacramental theology, not as a separate post-baptismal experience.

## 9. Eucharist

The Divine Liturgy is the centre of Orthodox Christian life, and the Eucharist is understood as the true Body and Blood of Christ, offered to the Father and received by the faithful (John Chrysostom, *Homilies on Matthew* 82; John of Damascus, *Exact Exposition* 4.13). Orthodoxy affirms a real, substantial presence of Christ in the eucharistic gifts but has historically avoided the scholastic philosophical terminology of transubstantiation, preferring to call the change a *metabole* (transformation) or *metousiosis* and to guard the mystery with apophatic restraint. The Divine Liturgy is the ongoing offering of the one sacrifice of Christ—Golgotha made present in the assembly—and the Eucharist is the medicine of immortality and the foretaste of the Kingdom. Communion is ordinarily reserved for the baptised and chrismated Orthodox faithful who have prepared through fasting and confession.

## 10. Papal Authority

The Orthodox Church rejects the Roman Catholic doctrine of papal supremacy and papal infallibility as innovations without grounding in the ecclesiology of the undivided Church. In the early Church, Rome held a primacy of honour as the ancient imperial capital and see of Peter and Paul, but this was a relational primacy within a collegial episcopate, not a jurisdictional monarchy over all other bishops (*Letter of the Eastern Patriarchs to Pope Pius IX*, 1848). The Council of Chalcedon (451, Canon 28) accorded Constantinople equal honour to Rome on the grounds of its imperial status—indicating that Roman primacy was at least partially a pragmatic arrangement. The Orthodox model of authority is conciliar: doctrinal definitions require reception by an Ecumenical Council and ultimately by the whole Church, and no single bishop—including the Ecumenical Patriarch—can define doctrine unilaterally.

## 11. Scripture and Tradition

Orthodox theology does not separate Scripture from Tradition; rather, Scripture is itself an expression of the Church's living Tradition, written under the inspiration of the Holy Spirit within the community of faith and properly understood only within that community (Georges Florovsky, *Bible, Church, Tradition*). Tradition is not primarily a collection of oral doctrines added to Scripture but the entire life of the Spirit in the Church: the liturgy, the creed, the icons, the councils, the Fathers—the "mind of the Church" (*phronema tes Ekklesias*). The Fathers are read not as infallible individual authorities but as the consensual witnesses to the faith of the Church; the "Patristic consensus" (*consensus Patrum*) on any given question carries great weight. Both Western positions—Catholic co-authority of Tradition with Scripture and Protestant *sola Scriptura*—are seen as failing to capture this organic unity.

## 12. Theosis (Divinisation)

Theosis is the central soteriological category of Eastern Orthodox theology: the ultimate end of the Christian life is participation in the divine nature (2 Pet 1:4), becoming "god by grace" while remaining human by nature (Maximus the Confessor, *Ambigua*; Gregory Palamas, *Triads*). Gregory Palamas (14th century) provided the theological framework for how this is possible without collapsing Creator and creature: God's essence (*ousia*) remains absolutely transcendent and unknowable, but his uncreated energies (*energeiai*)—God as he is in his operations toward creation—are genuinely shared with and participated in by the saints. Theosis is not the absorption of the human person into the divine (as in some Eastern monistic mysticisms) but the full realisation of personhood through loving union with the personal God, the Trinity. This process begins in baptism, deepens through the sacraments and ascetic struggle, and reaches its consummation in the Resurrection and the Age to Come.

## 13. Original Sin

The Orthodox understanding of original sin is shaped by the Greek Fathers rather than Augustine, and differs significantly from Western (Catholic and Protestant) formulations. Orthodoxy affirms that Adam's sin brought death, corruption, and a weakened nature into the world, which is transmitted to all his descendants (John of Damascus, *Exact Exposition* 2.30; Anastasius of Sinai). What is inherited is mortality and the tendency toward sin (*propatheiai*), not the guilt of Adam's personal sin—each person is responsible for their own actual sins, not for Adam's. This distinction means Orthodox theology does not share the strong Augustinian doctrine of inherited guilt, which shapes both the Catholic doctrine of the Immaculate Conception and the Protestant doctrine of total depravity in quite the same way. The solution to original sin is participation in the incorruptible life of the risen Christ through the Church and its mysteries.

## 14. Purgatory

The Orthodox Church does not teach the doctrine of purgatory as defined by Rome—that is, a distinct state of punitive satisfaction for temporal penalties of sins already forgiven. The Orthodox firmly rejected the Roman definition of purgatory at the Councils of Lyons (1274) and Florence (1438–1439), and the issue remains a significant point of ecumenical division. The Orthodox do, however, pray earnestly for the repose of the dead at the Divine Liturgy and on the Saturdays of the Dead, believing that God's mercy extends beyond death and that intercession may benefit those who have departed in hope. The theological basis is not the satisfaction of juridical penalties but the hope in God's infinite mercy and the power of the Church's communal love expressed in prayer, the nature of which God alone can actuate in ways unknown to us.

## 15. Hermeneutics

Orthodox hermeneutics is fundamentally *ecclesial*: Scripture is the book of the Church, born from within the community of faith and correctly interpreted only in its liturgical, patristic, and conciliar context. The Fathers—particularly those recognised as saints and teachers—are the primary guides to interpretation, and patristic exegesis (typological, allegorical, and spiritual readings alongside the literal) is honoured alongside modern historical-critical tools. The criterion for a correct interpretation is ultimately whether it coheres with the "mind of the Church" as expressed in the conciliar tradition, the creeds, the icons, and the liturgy—what Vincent of Lérins called what has been believed "everywhere, always, and by all" (*Commonitorium* 2.6). Iconography is itself a visual hermeneutic: the icon of Christ is regarded as a confession of the Incarnation in paint, and Orthodox theology holds together verbal and visual, written and lived expressions of the revealed truth.
