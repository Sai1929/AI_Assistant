# Protestant Tradition — Doctrinal Positions

This document summarises broadly shared Protestant positions on fifteen contested theological topics. Because Protestantism encompasses thousands of distinct denominations, positions are drawn from the historically normative Reformed and Lutheran confessional standards: the *Westminster Confession of Faith* (WCF, 1646), the *Augsburg Confession* (AC, 1530), the *Heidelberg Catechism* (HC, 1563), the *Second Helvetic Confession* (SHC, 1566), and the *39 Articles of the Church of England* (1571). Where significant intra-Protestant diversity exists, the range of views is noted.

---

## 1. Marian Dogmas

The Reformation broadly affirmed the ancient titles *Theotokos* (God-bearer) and Perpetual Virginity as pious traditions consistent with Scripture, though non-binding (Luther retained both; Calvin was more cautious). The doctrines of the Immaculate Conception and the Bodily Assumption are universally rejected by mainline Protestant bodies as lacking clear scriptural warrant and as additions to the deposit of faith (WCF 1.6: "nothing is to be added ... at any time"). Mary is honoured as a supremely faithful servant and exemplary disciple, but the invocation of Mary as Mediatrix or Co-Redemptrix is rejected as detracting from Christ's sole mediation (1 Tim 2:5; WCF 8.1). Protestant devotion to Mary is expressed through the praise of her obedient faith rather than liturgical veneration.

## 2. Sacraments

Most Protestant traditions recognise two dominically-instituted sacraments: Baptism and the Lord's Supper, based on the criterion that a sacrament must be commanded by Christ and accompanied by an external sign (WCF 27.1; HC Q.66; AC 13). The other five rites recognised by Rome are regarded as either pious ceremonies (confirmation, marriage, ordination) or were abolished (penance as a sacrament) or affirmed in a reduced sense (anointing). Lutheran theology holds that sacraments are means of grace that confer what they signify (*ex opere operato* language is avoided, but real efficacy is affirmed through faith). Reformed theology emphasises that the Holy Spirit seals and applies grace through the sacraments to those whom God has elected, so they are "signs and seals" of covenant grace (WCF 27.1).

## 3. Justification

*Sola fide*—justification by faith alone—is the material principle of the Protestant Reformation (WCF 11; AC 4; Luther: "the article by which the church stands or falls"). Justification is understood as a forensic declaration: God pronounces the sinner righteous on the basis of Christ's imputed righteousness, not on the basis of infused grace or meritorious acts (WCF 11.1). Faith is the alone instrument of justification, though it is never alone—it always produces genuine repentance and good works as its fruit (WCF 11.2; HC Q.64). Lutherans and Reformed agree on forensic justification by faith alone, though they differ on whether "sanctification" is a distinct subsequent benefit or integrated more closely with justification in a single event.

## 4. Predestination

Reformed (Calvinist) Protestantism holds to double predestination: God has from eternity elected some to salvation and passed over (reprobated) others, to the praise of his sovereign mercy and justice (WCF 3.3–7; Calvin, *Institutes* 3.21). This is understood not as arbitrary but as grounded in God's sovereign will, not foreknowledge of human merit (*supra-* vs *infra-lapsarian* debates notwithstanding). Lutheran theology affirms unconditional election to salvation but denies double predestination, leaving the damnation of the reprobate to their own free rejection of grace (AC, Epitome 11). Arminian and Wesleyan Protestants reject unconditional election altogether, affirming instead that God elects based on foreknown faith, and that saving grace is resistible and universal.

## 5. Scriptural Canon

Protestants follow the Hebrew canon of the Old Testament (22–24 books, equivalent to 39 in Protestant counting), excluding the deuterocanonical/apocryphal books accepted by Catholicism (WCF 1.2–3). Luther placed the Apocrypha in a separate section of his German Bible as "useful to read but not equal to Holy Scripture." The 39 Articles (VI) state that the deuterocanonicals "the Church doth read for example of life and instruction of manners; but yet doth it not apply them to establish any doctrine." The Protestant canon of 66 books is regarded as sufficient for all saving knowledge, consistent with the principle of *sola Scriptura*.

## 6. Ecclesiology

Protestant ecclesiology is shaped by the rejection of the papacy as the divinely ordained centre of unity. The Church is defined as the company of the elect known to God, made visible wherever the Word is rightly preached and the sacraments rightly administered (WCF 25.1–4; AC 7–8). Church governance varies widely: episcopalianism (bishops, as in Anglicanism and Methodism), presbyterianism (elected elders, as in Reformed churches), and congregationalism (local autonomy, as in Baptists). The Pope is denied any claim of universal jurisdiction over Christians, and the councils of the Church are held to be fallible, correctable by Scripture (WCF 31.3).

## 7. Eschatology

Mainstream Protestant confessions affirm the bodily resurrection, the Last Judgment, heaven, and hell (WCF 32–33; AC 17). Purgatory is uniformly rejected as "a fond thing vainly invented ... contrary to ... Scripture" (39 Articles 22) and as undermining the sufficiency of Christ's atoning work. Eschatological schemes vary considerably within Protestantism: amillennialism (dominant in Reformed tradition), premillennialism and postmillennialism (both well-represented), and dispensationalism (popular in Baptist and evangelical circles). All mainstream Protestant bodies affirm a personal, bodily, visible return of Christ and a final general resurrection and judgment.

## 8. Baptism

Baptism is a sacrament of initiation into the covenant community in which God pledges forgiveness of sins, union with Christ, and the gift of the Holy Spirit (WCF 28; HC Q.69–74; AC 9). The Reformed and Lutheran traditions practise infant baptism as the New Covenant counterpart to circumcision, initiated into the covenant community by grace prior to personal faith. Baptist and Anabaptist traditions reject infant baptism as lacking explicit New Testament warrant and insist on believer's (credobaptist) baptism as a profession of personal faith. Lutherans teach baptismal regeneration (*Luther's Small Catechism*), while the Reformed see baptism as a sign and seal that is effective only for the elect who exercise faith.

## 9. Eucharist

The Lord's Supper is the second Protestant sacrament, though its interpretation is sharply contested within Protestantism itself. Luther taught the real, bodily presence of Christ "in, with, and under" the bread and wine (*consubstantiation* or sacramental union; AC 10), rejecting both Rome's transubstantiation and Zwingli's memorialism. Zwingli held the Supper to be a memorial meal and sign of communal pledge, with no special presence of Christ beyond his spiritual presence wherever believers gather (Marburg Colloquy, 1529). Calvin taught a "spiritual real presence"—Christ's body and blood are truly received, but spiritually and by faith as the Holy Spirit lifts the believer's heart to heaven where Christ's body remains (HC Q.75–80). Most evangelical and Baptist churches follow broadly Zwinglian or Calvinian lines.

## 10. Papal Authority

Protestant traditions uniformly reject the papacy as a divinely instituted office and deny that the Bishop of Rome holds universal jurisdiction over the Church of Christ (WCF 25.6 calls the Pope "Antichrist"; 39 Articles 37 denies the Pope any "jurisdiction in this Realm of England"). The historical papacy is seen as a human development that departed from the collegial, servant-leadership model of the New Testament (Mt 20:25–28; 1 Pet 5:1–4). Luther's conflict with Rome crystallised around the question of authority: he denied that the Pope and councils are infallible and affirmed that councils have erred, making Scripture the sole final authority over the Church. Modern ecumenical dialogues have softened the rhetoric, and some Protestant bodies acknowledge a possible future role for the Bishop of Rome as a "minister of unity," but without jurisdictional supremacy.

## 11. Scripture and Tradition

*Sola Scriptura* is the formal principle of the Reformation: Scripture alone is the supreme, sufficient, and self-interpreting norm for faith and practice (WCF 1.2, 6, 10). Church councils, creeds, and confessions are authoritative only insofar as they agree with Scripture and may always be corrected by it (WCF 31.3; Luther at the Diet of Worms: "my conscience is captive to the Word of God"). This does not mean Scripture is interpreted in isolation from the Church's tradition (*nuda scriptura*); confessional Protestants regard the ecumenical creeds and Reformation confessions as subordinate standards that express the true sense of Scripture. Tradition is thus a secondary guide and hermeneutical lens, not an independent source of revelation coordinate with Scripture.

## 12. Theosis (Divinisation)

Classical Reformed and Lutheran theology does not typically use the language of *theosis*, emphasising instead the distinction between Creator and creature (the *archetypal/ectypal* distinction). Salvation is framed principally as justification, adoption, and sanctification—being declared righteous, made children of God, and progressively conformed to Christ's image by the Holy Spirit (WCF 13; HC Q.86). Contemporary Protestant scholarship has renewed engagement with theosis language: Lutheran scholars such as Tuomo Mannermaa argue that Luther's theology implies a genuine indwelling of Christ (*in ipsa fide Christus adest*). Many Reformed theologians affirm union with Christ as the ground of all saving benefits, which comes close to Eastern conceptions of participation, though the ontological framing differs.

## 13. Original Sin

Protestantism holds a robust doctrine of original sin, affirming that Adam's fall plunged the entire human race into guilt, corruption, and spiritual inability (WCF 6; AC 2; HC Q.7–11). Total depravity—a key element of Reformed theology—means not that every person is as sinful as possible, but that sin affects every faculty of human nature, including the will, so that without divine grace no one is able to turn to God (WCF 9.3). Lutheranism similarly affirms that original sin is a real corruption of nature, not merely the absence of original righteousness. Arminian and Wesleyan traditions qualify total depravity with prevenient grace, a universal work of the Spirit that partially restores human freedom so that all can respond to the Gospel.

## 14. Purgatory

Purgatory is rejected across all Protestant traditions as a human invention without scriptural foundation, and as implying that Christ's atonement is insufficient to cleanse the believer fully from sin (WCF 32.1; 39 Articles 22; Luther's *95 Theses*, 1517). The scriptural text most commonly cited in its favour (2 Macc 12:46) is in a book Protestants do not regard as canonical. At death, the souls of the righteous pass immediately into God's presence (2 Cor 5:8; Phil 1:23), where they await the resurrection in conscious blessedness—a state sometimes called the "intermediate state." Good works, indulgences, and masses for the dead are therefore not practised, as the sufficiency of Christ's once-for-all sacrifice (*ephapax*, Heb 7:27) renders such practices unnecessary and misleading.

## 15. Hermeneutics

The Protestant hermeneutical principle *Scripture interprets Scripture* (*analogia Scripturae*) holds that clearer passages shed light on obscure ones, and that the overall unity of the Bible provides the framework for understanding any individual text (WCF 1.9). The literal-historical sense is primary; the Reformers moved away from the fourfold medieval method of allegory while not abandoning typological reading that sees the Old Testament as fulfilled in Christ. Individual Christians have the right and responsibility to read Scripture for themselves in their own languages—a conviction that drove Luther to translate the Bible into German and that motivated the translation projects of Tyndale and the KJV translators. Modern Protestant scholarship embraces grammatical-historical exegesis alongside canonical and theological reading strategies, seeking to hear Scripture's message in its original context while applying it to the contemporary church.
